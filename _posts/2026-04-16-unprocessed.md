Like a lot of people in 2020, I had more time to sit and think about existential problems like where I got my food. I stumbled across the book [Unprocessed](https://www.goodreads.com/en/book/show/23315857-unprocessed) and fell in love with the idea. I'm no stranger to trying weird diets (Keto, Paleo, Fasting, etc) out of just general curiosity so I thought I'd take a stab at making my own diet. I ended up with the following:

## Unprocessed

### Premise

Eating locally, unrefined, understandable ingredients that are sourced provincially or nationally. Traceability and understanding are key elements. If you can’t make it, you can’t eat it.

### Rules

- All food must be able to be created by hand from its core elements.
- All food must be traced to its point of origin. If not known entirely, it must be attempted and bought with trust it is an ethical product.
- All meat will be bought in bulk and locally. Preferably thawed. Seafood should be either canned, frozen, or fresh from the Atlantic Ocean.
- Food should ideally be organic if within 20% of the non-organic substitute. Exception: “The Dirty Dozen” (see below).

#### The Dirty Dozen (basically food with heavy pesticide usage that you eat the skin of)

	- Strawberry
	- Spinach
	- Kale
	- Nectarines
	- Apples
	- Grapes
	- Peaches
	- Cherries
	- Pears
	- Tomatoes
	- Celery
	- Potatoes

#### Exemptions (within reason) apply to:

- Major events where other food is provided (exempt on: Birthday, Easter Dinner, Thanksgiving Dinner, Christmas Dinner)
- Culturally significant meals where insult could be conveyed to another
- A food or meal offered that contains something unique or never eaten before (for tasting purposes only)
- Food that existed in my pantry prior

- Buy spices or minerals that are more efficient and sustainable (e.g., sea salt vs. chemical salt). Spices should be sourced.
- Eating should always be done at a place designated for that purpose (e.g., table).
- Coffee should be traced and fair trade. Ideally organic. This applies to tea as well.
- Beans and legumes should be bought dried if possible.
- Sweeteners / desserts will include dates, maple syrup, and honey.


What followed was 3 months of exploration into my local food system. I got a CSA (Community Supported Agriculture) for veggies and meat, I always read the packaging to the products I was buying, and I really went out of my way to find out what restaurants supported local farmers. It was a really rewarding experience, and while I never stayed true to it long term I learned a lot in the process.
